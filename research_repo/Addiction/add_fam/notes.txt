Notes for this influence:

Addiction → Family Values = -0.21

Substance addiction destabilizes family structures and weakens the transmission of family values through several pathways:

- Household instability
  - Addiction introduces financial strain, unpredictability, and conflict into the household.
  - Priorities often shift from long-term responsibilities to short-term substance maintenance.

- Parental capacity deterioration
  - Intoxication, withdrawal cycles, and declining health reduce caregiving consistency.
  - Children experience reduced supervision, emotional availability, and structure.

- Family conflict and breakdown
  - Addiction is strongly associated with:
    - divorce and separation
    - domestic conflict
    - neglect and abuse risk
  - Trust within the household erodes as addiction becomes the dominant organizing force in family life.

- Intergenerational transmission
  - Children raised in high-addiction environments face higher exposure to trauma and unstable attachment patterns.
  - These conditions weaken the transmission of norms, traditions, and family stability across generations.

Empirical research consistently links substance use disorders to family disruption and impaired parental functioning.

The effect is strong but not maximal because:

- Many families maintain cohesion despite addiction in one member.
- Extended family, community, or religious networks can buffer some of the damage.
- Recovery and treatment can partially restore family relationships and values.

**Shapes**

- Soft logistic decline

  - Low addiction prevalence → most families remain stable; values create resistance at first.
  - Moderate addiction prevalence → noticeable increases in family conflict, instability, and breakdown.
  - High addiction prevalence → family structures deteriorate more rapidly as addiction becomes normalized within communities and multiple household members are affected.

Shape note: Family systems tolerate isolated addiction cases, but once addiction becomes common within a social network or community the ability of families to maintain consistent norms and structure declines more rapidly.

- Mild threshold curve (possibly with a kink indicating early resistance)

  - Healthy families with strong values may be able to resist early addiction pressures through caring social networks, financial and emotional support, and spiritual or cultural guidance.

  - Family systems are somewhat shock-absorbing. One addicted individual does not always collapse the family unit, but once instability crosses a certain threshold deterioration accelerates. In severe cases, a single deeply addicted family member can reach a tipping point where the entire household begins to unravel.
  
 **Higher-order Note:**
 Once family cohesion deteriorates, secondary effects propagate into mental health, education stability, and crime risk.
